# Book Notes Output Format

This document provides detailed examples of the markdown output format generated by the book-notes-extractor skill.

## Overall Structure

```markdown
# [Book Title]

**Author:** [Author Name]
**Language:** [Language Code]
**Publisher:** [Publisher] (if available)

---

## Book Overview

[AI-generated overview of the entire book - 1-2 paragraphs covering main themes, structure, and key contributions]

---

## Reading Notes

[Chapter-by-chapter notes follow...]
```

## Chapter Note Structure

Each chapter follows this template:

```markdown
### Chapter N: [Chapter Title]

**Summary:**
[2-3 sentence summary capturing the chapter's main point and how it fits into the book's overall argument]

**Key Points:**

#### 1. [Theme/Topic Name] ⭐⭐⭐
[Detailed explanation in 2-3 sentences about this theme or concept. Should be substantive and provide clear understanding of the idea.]

**Example:** [Concrete example from the chapter text that illustrates this point. Should be specific, not generic.]

**Related:** [Cross-references to other chapters that discuss similar themes, if applicable]

---

#### 2. [Another Theme] ⭐⭐
[Explanation of this supporting concept...]

**Example:** [Specific example...]

---

[Continue for remaining key points...]

---
```

## Importance Ratings

The three-star rating system indicates the relative importance of each key point:

- **⭐** Supporting detail - Provides context or supplementary information
- **⭐⭐** Important concept - Key idea that supports the main argument
- **⭐⭐⭐** Core argument - Central thesis or critical concept for understanding the chapter

## Example: Complete Chapter Note

Here's a complete example for a hypothetical psychology book chapter:

```markdown
### Chapter 3: The Psychology of Crowds

**Summary:**
This chapter examines how individual behavior changes dramatically in group settings. Le Bon argues that crowds develop a collective mind that overrides individual reasoning, leading to impulsive and sometimes destructive behavior. The chapter establishes the psychological mechanisms that transform rational individuals into an emotional collective entity.

**Key Points:**

#### 1. Loss of Individual Responsibility ⭐⭐⭐
When individuals join a crowd, they experience a diffusion of personal responsibility. The anonymity of the group creates a psychological shield where individuals feel their actions cannot be traced back to them personally. This loss of accountability removes normal social inhibitions and moral constraints that guide individual behavior.

**Example:** The chapter describes how peaceful protesters can transform into rioters when surrounded by large crowds, engaging in destructive behavior they would never consider as individuals. The larger the crowd, the stronger the feeling of anonymity and reduced personal accountability.

**Related:** Chapter 5 discusses leadership's role in directing crowd behavior; Chapter 8 examines historical examples of crowd psychology

---

#### 2. Emotional Contagion ⭐⭐⭐
Emotions spread rapidly through crowds like a contagious disease. Le Bon identifies that crowds amplify and transmit feelings through a process of mutual suggestion, where each member both influences and is influenced by those around them. This creates a feedback loop that intensifies the dominant emotion exponentially.

**Example:** In theater crowds, a few people laughing can trigger widespread laughter throughout the audience, even among those who didn't find the joke particularly funny. Similarly, panic in one section of a crowd can rapidly spread to create mass hysteria.

**Related:** Chapter 4 explores the biological basis of emotional contagion

---

#### 3. Intellectual Simplification ⭐⭐
Crowds cannot engage with complex or nuanced ideas. Instead, they respond only to simple, extreme concepts presented in vivid imagery. Subtle arguments, logical reasoning, and careful distinctions disappear in the crowd mind, replaced by absolute positions and dramatic contrasts between good and evil.

**Example:** Political speeches to large crowds succeed by using simple slogans and powerful metaphors rather than detailed policy analysis. The most effective crowd leaders use repetition of basic ideas and evocative imagery rather than rational persuasion.

---

#### 4. Heightened Suggestibility ⭐⭐
Individuals in crowds become highly susceptible to suggestion and lose critical thinking capacity. The crowd mind is credulous, accepting fantastic claims and impossible promises without skepticism. This suggestibility makes crowds easily manipulated by charismatic leaders who understand crowd psychology.

**Example:** Historical witch hunts demonstrate crowd suggestibility, where entire communities became convinced of impossible claims about supernatural powers based on minimal evidence and mass suggestion.

**Related:** Chapter 6 examines how leaders exploit crowd suggestibility

---

#### 5. Regression to Primitive Instincts ⭐
The crowd mind represents a step backward in psychological evolution. Crowds bypass higher rational faculties and operate through primitive instincts, similar to how Le Bon characterizes "inferior forms of evolution." This regression explains why crowds are conservative, resistant to change, and attracted to authority.

**Example:** Religious crowds often display ritualistic behaviors and unquestioning devotion to traditional practices, resisting any innovations or reforms that conflict with established customs.

---

#### 6. Exaggeration and Absolutism ⭐
Crowds cannot tolerate doubt or uncertainty. Every sentiment in the crowd mind is magnified to its extreme form. Moderate positions disappear - suspicion becomes certainty, antipathy becomes hatred, and admiration becomes worship. This creates an environment where nuance is impossible.

**Example:** In political crowds, opponents are not merely wrong but evil; supporters are not merely correct but heroic. The crowd eliminates all middle ground and forces every issue into black-and-white terms.

---
```

## Footer

At the end of the complete notes:

```markdown
**Generated by book-notes-extractor**
**Source:** /path/to/original/book.epub
**Settings:** 6 points per chapter, medium detail level
```

## Notes on Cross-References

Cross-references should be:
- **Specific**: Mention chapter numbers and themes, not just "see other chapters"
- **Meaningful**: Only reference chapters with actual thematic connections
- **Bidirectional**: If Chapter 3 references Chapter 5, Chapter 5 should reference Chapter 3
- **Thematic**: Focus on conceptual connections, not just keyword overlap

Good cross-reference:
```markdown
**Related:** Chapter 7 explores crowd behavior in digital environments; Chapter 9 examines leadership techniques for managing crowds
```

Poor cross-reference:
```markdown
**Related:** See other chapters for more information
```

## Handling Special Content

### LaTeX Formulas

Preserve formulas exactly as they appear in the source:

```markdown
#### Statistical Analysis ⭐⭐
The chapter introduces the central limit theorem, which states that $\bar{X} \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$ for sufficiently large sample sizes.

**Example:** For a population with mean $\mu = 100$ and standard deviation $\sigma = 15$, a sample of size $n = 30$ will have a sampling distribution with standard error:

$$
SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{15}{\sqrt{30}} \approx 2.74
$$
```

### Images and Figures

Reference images by description since they can't be embedded in markdown notes:

```markdown
#### Visual Perception Principles ⭐⭐
The chapter presents the Gestalt principles of visual grouping...

**Example:** [Figure 3.2] demonstrates the proximity principle, showing how elements placed close together are perceived as a group even when they differ in color or shape.
```

## Adapting for Different Detail Levels

### Low Detail
- 3-4 key points per chapter
- 1-2 sentence explanations
- Brief examples or no examples
- Focus only on ⭐⭐⭐ core concepts

### Medium Detail (Default)
- 5-8 key points per chapter
- 2-3 sentence explanations
- Concrete example for each point
- Mix of ⭐⭐⭐, ⭐⭐, and ⭐ points

### High Detail
- 8-12 key points per chapter
- 3-4 sentence explanations with deeper analysis
- Multiple examples per point when relevant
- More extensive cross-references
- Include supporting details (⭐) more liberally
